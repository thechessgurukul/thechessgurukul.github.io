Step 2: Understanding the "Three Flavors" of Ratings ‚è±Ô∏è

One of the most important things for parents to understand is that FIDE maintains three separate rating lists. A player does not just have one single rating; they can earn a specific rating for Standard (Classical), Rapid, and Blitz chess.

Standard ratings are considered the "Gold Standard" for serious improvement and earning titles like Grandmaster. Rapid ratings are very common in India due to the popularity of "One-Day" Sunday tournaments, while Blitz ratings track a player's performance in lightning-fast games. It is important to note that getting a rating in one category does not automatically give you a rating in the others. You must meet the qualification criteria separately for each of these three formats.

Step 4: The 5-Game Rule üî¢

There is often confusion about how many games are needed. To get your first rating in any category, you must meet these specific criteria:

  1. Play 5 games against players who already have a FIDE rating in that specific category. (Note: You can "pool" these games from different tournaments over a 26-month period).
  2. Score at least 0.5 points (one draw or win) against those rated players.
  3. Achieve a Performance of 1400+: Since 2024, the "Rating Floor" is 1400. If your calculated performance is 1350, you stay "Unrated" until you cross the 1400 mark.
Note: While you only play 5 real games, FIDE‚Äôs internal math now includes two "imaginary" draws against 1800-rated opponents. This ensures your initial rating is stable and accurately reflects your skill level from day one.

Step 5: Tournament Survival Guide ü•§

Classical games can last 4 hours, and Rapid tournaments often involve 9 games in a single day. Preparation is key:

  • The "Chess Diet": Pack fruit, nuts, and plenty of water. Avoid heavy or oily meals between rounds as they can cause energy crashes.
  • The "Cool Down": After a game, give the child 10 minutes of "quiet time" or a short walk before discussing the moves. The brain needs to reset for the next round.

The journey to becoming a rated chess player is as much about character building as it is about strategy. It teaches patience, resilience, and the value of hard work. As your child embarks on this path, remember that the rating is just a reflection of their current progress‚Äîthe real victory lies in the love for the game and the discipline they develop along the way.
